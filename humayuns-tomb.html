Humayun's Tomb

The Inspiration Behind the Taj Mahal

Introduction

Humayun's Tomb is one of Delhi’s most magnificent historical monuments and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Built in 1570, it was the first garden-tomb in India and inspired the later construction of the Taj Mahal.

The monument marks the tomb of the Mughal Emperor Humayun and stands as a symbol of Mughal architectural brilliance.

Historical Background

After Emperor Humayun died in 1556, his wife Empress Bega Begum commissioned the construction of this grand tomb. It was designed by Persian architects, combining Persian and Indian architectural styles.

This structure introduced the concept of the Charbagh (four-part garden) in India, which later became a key feature of Mughal architecture.

Architectural Features

Humayun’s Tomb is built using red sandstone with white marble detailing. The tomb stands on a large raised platform and features a double dome structure.

  • Symmetrical Persian-style garden layout
  • Beautiful marble dome
  • Arched entrances
  • Intricate lattice (jaali) work
  • Geometric pathways and water channels

The monument beautifully balances simplicity and grandeur, making it one of Delhi’s most visited heritage sites.

UNESCO Recognition

Humayun’s Tomb was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993. It is considered one of the finest examples of Mughal garden-tomb architecture in India.
